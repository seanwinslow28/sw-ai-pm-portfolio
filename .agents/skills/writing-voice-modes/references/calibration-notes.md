# Voice Calibration Notes — Sean Winslow

> Distilled from a 9-question interview and 4 writing exercises (March 8, 2026).
> This is the synthesis, not the transcript. Use alongside SKILL.md when fine-tuning voice output.

---

## Interview Findings

### Q1: What makes your writing feel like yours?
**Dry humor is #1.** Then: quick setup → rollercoaster ride with silly references → witty ending or callback to the beginning. Short punchy sentences to build up to the finale.

### Q2: What kind of "silly references"?
**Mix of pop culture and hyper-specific personal anecdotes.** Movies, TV, memes, music, cultural events used in unexpected contexts. Personal stories so specific the reader pictures them without shared experience — they chuckle and think about it as the day goes on.

### Q3: How much voice in professional contexts?
**Dialed back but recognizable (option B).** Structure and dry humor survive. Silly references reserved for comfortable audiences. Sarcasm is always present regardless of context.

### Q4: Sentence rhythm preference?
**Naturally alternates, leans toward flowing connected sentences (option B).** Loves the feel of short staccato buildup (option A) but defaults to longer flowing sentences linked by commas and dashes. Shifts to staccato for impact.

### Q5: How do you deliver serious points?
**Sneak up through humor (option A).** Hard to take anything seriously, which comes out in writing. Alternate route: serious point first, then jokey release valve. "Here's the deal... then we'll burn it all."

### Q6: Specificity instinct?
**Both analogy and numbers, but analogy comes first (option C).** Paints the sensory picture, then drops the number as confirmation. "Dashboard screeching like Pink Floyd's Great Gig In The Sky... 847 errors in ninety seconds."

### Q7: Hardest mode to write in?
**Vonnegut's Minimalist Absurdist.** Feels like "reading a children's novel with adult undertones." Connects to the refrain/deflation mechanic ("How did I get here?" = "So it goes" energy) but not to sustained short-sentence austerity. Needs the runway to build sensory cascades before the short hit lands.

### Q8: Actual voice samples provided
Sean wrote the origin story unprompted — mushrooms on New Year's, nine years of corporate freelancing, Staten Island ferry commute, bathroom epiphany with Studio Ghibli portraits. Raw voice confirmed all interview findings: sensory detail, self-deprecation, pop culture anchoring, metaphor stacking, flowing sentences, and the hard cut.

### Q9: Personal vs. audience voice?
**Same voice, different polish.** Notes are more chaotic. Also writes in poem format (Pastey Boy) and screenwriting cut-to format. The *cut to* is a structural signature that crosses all formats.

## Mode Ranking (Native → Stretch)

1. **Sedaris-Thompson hybrid** — Home base. Self-deprecating humor + hyper-specific details + self-implication + quest structure.
2. **Kerouac** — Natural sentence engine. Flowing rhythm, sensory cascading, dash breath marks. Already his default gear.
3. **Vonnegut** — Punctuation toolkit only. Refrains, flat collisions, and devastating one-liners deployed in bursts. NOT sustained for whole pieces.

## Key Calibration Discoveries

### Sean's closers are his strongest move
Every exercise, he replaced the AI-generated ending with something better. This is his most reliable instinct — trust it, invest in it, build toward it.

### One strong reference earns it — three is self-indulgence
The bathroom smell lesson: AI wrote three variations on the bathroom smell in one paragraph. Sean flagged it immediately. One vivid sensory reference is enough. Repeating the bit means you don't trust it landed. This is the primary "Bad Sean" anti-pattern.

### Sensory detail over logistics
When rewriting the commute passage, Sean dropped the specific train lines (ferry → 4/5 → 6) and replaced them with the *feeling* of the commute (lost souls, ship of the damned, sheep). He uses specificity for comedy and atmosphere, not for directions.

### The rule of three always pivots on the third
Two concrete/funny items, then the third pivots to genuine emotion. This is not occasional — it's structural. "Skills, coffee, and for once in my life, a glimmer of hope." The pivot is never signposted.

### Metaphor stacking works when they describe the same thing
Sean stacked three metaphors for "commute" (ship of the damned → sheep to slaughter → hamster wheel) and it worked because each one escalated while describing the same mundane reality. The danger: stacking metaphors that describe *different* things = chaos.

### Tense shifts in refrains create resolution
"I began again" (past, repeated) → "I have begun" (present perfect, once). The shift from loop to resolution is Sean's version of the Vonnegut mechanic — his refrains have a direction. Vonnegut's are circular.

### Self-deprecation is structural, not decorative
Sean makes himself the biggest fool in every scene. This isn't insecurity — it's an earned right to observe others without cruelty. It's also what prevents his Thompson-style system criticism from becoming sanctimonious.

### The screenwriting background is load-bearing
The *cut to*, the hard juxtaposition, the scene-kick ALL CAPS, the comedic timing — these come from screenwriting instincts, not prose instincts. Any voice mode that strips these out will feel wrong.

## What Doesn't Work for Sean

- Sustained short-sentence austerity (Vonnegut as full mode)
- Clinical or flat delivery without sensory runway
- Self-deprecation that slides into self-pity
- Repeating the same sensory detail more than once per passage
- Explaining the joke or signposting the emotional pivot
- Generic references ("a restaurant" vs. "a mid-priced sushi chain in Kalamazoo")
- Closers that summarize instead of transform

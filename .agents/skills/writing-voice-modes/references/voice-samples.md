## Raw Voice (Unedited Sean)

### Hyper-Specific Anecdote + Self-Deprecation
> "I haven't squinted this hard since I got drunk in the streets of Phuket on a bucket of vodka redbulls and stumbled into a local bathhouse"

**Why it works:** Hyper-specific (Phuket, bucket of vodka redbulls, bathhouse), slightly self-incriminating, the reader pictures it instantly without shared experience. Sedaris mechanics with a more adventurous, worldly flavor.

### Hard Cut / Deflation (Screenwriting Format)
> "I think I'll be taking it easy tonight. I could use some R&R"
> *cut to*
> "SHOVE THE BEER BONG UP MY ASS AND BLOW COKE IN MY EYES. IT'S SATURDAY, BABY"

**Why it works:** Hard juxtaposition between expectation and reality. The *cut to* is Sean's structural signature — he loves the gap between what's expected and what actually happens. Thompson's ALL CAPS for the scene kick.

### Humor as Trojan Horse (Werewolf Birthday Message)
> "Haha yea, it's kind of like a person, mid transformation, turning into a werewolf... Only instead of running around at night with a hunger for flesh, you start noticing you're tired by 6 pm and you think about taxes more often"

**Why it works:** Epic setup (werewolf transformation) → mundane punchline (tired by 6 pm, taxes). The humor comes from the deflation. Grand framing, human landing. Consistent pattern across all his writing.

### Compression / Micro-Format (Poem)
> **Pastey Boy**
> The melanin
> In my skin
> Is slim

**Why it works:** Three lines doing maximum work — setup, detail, punchline, all landing on a rhyme. Same deflation pattern in a different format.

### Serious Point with Release Valve
> "Here's the deal. We have to do this, this, and this. Then we can do that, that, and that... then we'll burn it all, throw our hands in the air, and question everything."

**Why it works:** Even when going serious-first, Sean can't resist the release valve at the end. Gravity never sits uninterrupted for long.

## Exercise Rewrites (Sean Correcting AI Output)

These are the most valuable samples — they show where Sean's instincts override the mode templates.

### Domestic Observer Closer (Sedaris Mode)
**AI wrote:** "I haven't organized a media file since."
**Sean rewrote:** "I no longer organize media files. Agents do."

**Why it's better:** Two short sentences. The second is two words. "Agents do" is funny, slightly menacing, and callbacks to the AI theme without explaining itself. Sean's closers are sharper than Sedaris — he doesn't linger.

### Gonzo Technical — Commute Passage (Thompson Mode)
**AI wrote:** "By 7:45 the next morning I was on the A train heading to Midtown..."
**Sean rewrote:** "By 7:15 the next morning, I was slowly migrating onto the Staten Island ferry with the rest of the lost souls stuck in the same pattern. Our baggy, bloodshot eyes staring into the void as we collectively migrate towards the ship of the damned. Sheep heading to the slaughterhouse we call our office. New year. New resolutions. New ways to trick ourselves into happiness. The hamster wheel rolls on."

**Why it's better:** Three escalating metaphors (ship of the damned → sheep to slaughter → hamster wheel) that all describe the same mundane thing: a commute. Thompson's escalation loop running on Sean's engine. Then the short punchy sentences at the end ("New year. New resolutions. New ways to trick ourselves into happiness.") build staccato momentum before the deflation closer.

### Beat Flow Closer (Kerouac Mode)
**AI wrote:** "That was then. Now I build agents in my terminal at 5 AM with cold coffee and a mass of skills I didn't have eighteen months ago. The ferry still runs. I don't ride it anymore."
**Sean rewrote:** "That was then. Now I wake up before my alarm. Fuelled by the new found skills that didn't exist 18 months ago, fresh brewed coffee, and for once in my life, a glimmer of hope. I hear the ferry horn blast from across the Hudson. It still shepards the herd to and fro ... but I no longer rub elbows with sheep."

**Why it's better:** Rule of three with emotional pivot ("skills, coffee, and for once in my life, a glimmer of hope" — two concrete, third pivots to feeling). Sensory anchoring (ferry horn blast). Callback to "sheep" from the Thompson exercise. The closing transforms the earlier image rather than restating it.

### Sensory Detail Correction (Kerouac Mode)
**AI wrote:** "their coffee cups gripped like rosaries"
**Sean rewrote:** "their travel mugs filled with caffeine and hair of the dog. An elixir of motivation and a way to cope."

**Why it's better:** "An elixir of motivation and a way to cope" holds two truths simultaneously — the Kerouac dual-meaning technique. Also avoids repeating "coffee" (Sean flagged over-referencing the same sensory detail as an anti-pattern).

### Minimalist Absurdist Closer (Vonnegut Mode)
**AI wrote:** "It sounds the same as it always did. I sound different."
**Sean rewrote:** "It sounds the same as it always did. I sound different... I have begun."

**Why it's better:** Tense shift turns the refrain from a loop into a resolution. Every "I began again" before it sounds like restarting the same cycle. "I have begun" breaks the cycle — three words that rewrite every prior instance. The Vonnegut mechanic at its highest level: the refrain changes meaning without (almost) changing words. Sean's version has direction. Vonnegut's is circular. Sean's escapes.

## Full Exercise Passages (Final Versions)

### Domestic Observer Mode — Applied
> For nine years I commuted two hours each way to organize media files in the windowless basement of a building so old it smelled like it was working toward its second millennium. New York Life Insurance Company. The name alone should've been the tell — I was insuring someone else's life while mine collected dust between hard drives and hollow water cooler conversations. I smiled through them like a man who'd been smiling so long his face forgot it was a choice.
>
> I had three animated shorts, five scripts, and thousands of dollars distributed across the pockets of people who swore they could get my work in front of the right people. The right people were apparently busy. Screenwriting was supposed to be the exit, but the more I learned about Hollywood, the more it sounded like the same basement with better lighting. Writers rooms had shrunk to fit nepo babies and the friends of friends of friends, and the golden age of the evergreen sitcom had packed its bags sometime around 2015 without telling anyone where it was going.
>
> Then one Tuesday in March — I remember it was a Tuesday because the bathroom stalls at New York Life were marginally less repulsive on Tuesdays — I was scrolling Instagram while perched on a toilet older than my freelance career, and I saw it. People were posting pictures of themselves in the style of Studio Ghibli. ChatGPT had learned to draw. I'd seen the Will Smith spaghetti video. I was a non-believer. But sitting there, something shifted. It was the kind of epiphany you'd expect to happen on a mountaintop or in a therapist's office, not in a bathroom stall that smelled like it was staging an intervention of its own.
>
> I no longer organize media files. Agents do.

### Gonzo Technical Mode — Applied
> January 1st, 2024. High on mushrooms, staring at myself in a bathroom mirror at — I want to say 2 AM but time had stopped being reliable about three hours earlier. I made a declaration to the stranger in the reflection: this is the year I begin again. The stranger did not look convinced.
>
> By 7:15 the next morning, I was slowly migrating onto the Staten Island ferry with the rest of the lost souls stuck in the same pattern. Our baggy, bloodshot eyes staring into the void as we collectively migrate towards the ship of the damned. Sheep heading to the slaughterhouse we call our office. New year. New resolutions. New ways to trick ourselves into happiness. The hamster wheel rolls on.
>
> The escape plan was screenwriting. I had five scripts, three animated shorts, and a growing portfolio of receipts from consultants, coverage services, and festival submission fees — $4,200 across 2022 and 2023 alone, paid to people whose primary skill was maintaining eye contact while saying "it's all about getting it in front of the right people." The right people were apparently on a different planet. Meanwhile, Hollywood's writers went on strike over AI, which I found amusing in the way you find something amusing right before it becomes your entire life. I'd seen the Will Smith spaghetti video. Computers couldn't do what humans do.
>
> MARCH 25TH, 2025. New York Life bathroom stall. Instagram open. Studio Ghibli portraits flooding every feed. ChatGPT-4o image generation had dropped and the internet lost its collective mind. I was sitting on a toilet that had seen more human despair than most therapists, scrolling through AI-generated art with the quiet horror of a man who had just bet his career on the wrong horse and the horse was now being rendered in watercolor by a machine.
>
> I charged forward. Screenwriting podcasts replaced by AI tutorials. Writing exercises replaced by vibecoding sessions. A profession called "Product Management" materialized in my vocabulary like a cheat code I'd been walking past for a decade. By November I had the job. By March I was building autonomous agents in my terminal at 5 AM.
>
> The bathroom epiphanies have not stopped. I'm choosing not to examine that pattern.

### Beat Flow Mode — Applied
> I was high on mushrooms staring at my own face in the mirror — the face of a man who had been commuting to the same windowless basement for nine years and organizing the same media files in the same dead air of a building so old it had given up pretending to smell like anything other than what it was — and I said to myself, to the stranger behind the glass with the bloodshot eyes and the jaw clenched tight like a fist that forgot how to open, I said this is the year, this is the one, and the stranger just stared back like he'd heard it all before because he had.
>
> And the ferry the next morning — the Staten Island ferry packed with the same lost souls shuffling aboard in their winter coats and their dead eyes and their travel mugs filled with caffeine and hair of the dog, an elixir of motivation and a way to cope — all of us migrating across that gray water toward Manhattan like a slow parade of the professionally damned, and I remember thinking even then, even in the middle of the resolution and the mushroom afterglow and the desperate wanting, I remember thinking this feels exactly like last year.
>
> Nine years of fluorescent light and lanyard swipes and water cooler small talk about nothing — nine years of animated shorts nobody watched and scripts nobody read and thousands of dollars hemorrhaged into the pockets of consultants who promised the right people and delivered the same empty room — nine years of screenwriting podcasts and festival submission fees and the slow erosion of believing your own talent means anything when the door won't open — and then one morning in March, sitting in a bathroom stall at New York Life Insurance Company, scrolling Instagram on a toilet older than my ambition, I saw the Studio Ghibli portraits flooding every feed and felt something crack open in my chest like a door I didn't know was there — and I walked through it and I kept walking and I never turned around.
>
> That was then. Now I wake up before my alarm. Fuelled by the new found skills that didn't exist 18 months ago, fresh brewed coffee, and for once in my life, a glimmer of hope. I hear the ferry horn blast from across the Hudson. It still shepards the herd to and fro ... but I no longer rub elbows with sheep.

### Minimalist Absurdist Mode — Applied
> Here is what happened. I organized media files in a basement for nine years. The building was 150 years old. It smelled like it. I commuted two hours each way on the Staten Island ferry. So did everyone else. We did not discuss this.
>
> I had three animated shorts and five screenplays and several thousand dollars' worth of advice from people who knew the right people. The right people never materialized. I began again.
>
> In 2024 I saw the Will Smith spaghetti video and decided AI was not a threat. Writers went on strike over it. I found this amusing. I began again.
>
> In March 2025 I was sitting on a toilet at New York Life Insurance Company scrolling Instagram. Everyone was a Studio Ghibli character. ChatGPT had learned to draw. I had not learned to draw in nine years of art school. The machine learned in an afternoon. I began again.
>
> I replaced screenwriting podcasts with AI tutorials. I replaced writing exercises with vibecoding. I learned a phrase called "Product Management." By November I had a job doing it. I was thirty-three years old. Most product managers start at twenty-four.
>
> I now wake up at 4:45 AM on purpose. I build things in a terminal. I talk to machines and the machines talk back. Sometimes they are more helpful than the consultants were. They are always cheaper. I began again.
>
> The ferry still runs. I hear it from across the Hudson some mornings, if the coffee hasn't kicked in yet and the apartment is quiet enough. It sounds the same as it always did. I sound different... I have begun.

### Sample 1 — PM role observation [Twitter long-form / LinkedIn at 60% dial]

**AI wrote:** "AI is fundamentally changing how product managers work. The ones who learn to use it well will have a significant advantage in the years ahead."

**Sean rewrote:** "The PM role has changed and I'm along for the ride. I no longer have to bother a dev trying to eat dinner with their family to make the header bold and a few pixels larger. A slash command and intent engineering covers that and so. much. more."

**Signature moves:** Domestic Defamiliarizer (PM work = "bothering a dev at dinner"); Hyper-Specific Anecdote (bold header + a few pixels larger — the most embarrassing real PM ticket ever filed); Vonnegut compression closer ("so. much. more." — period-spaced staccato, three words doing the work of a paragraph); Self-Deprecation as Structure ("along for the ride" — observer, not driver).

**Why it works:** the AI version makes an abstract claim about a class of people. Sean's version makes a specific confession about himself, and the confession does the load-bearing work the abstract claim was reaching for. "So. much. more." is the Vonnegut punctuation toolkit in burst form — three words, three periods, devastating.

### Sample 2 — Substack intro on the Ghibli moment [Substack, full intensity]

**AI wrote:** "I'll never forget the day I realized AI was going to change everything about my career."

**Sean rewrote:** "A lifetime of drawing and understanding the craft of Miyazaki's artistic techniques was just spit out on my friends phone in a matter of seconds. A lot of creatives having their own version of that moment were probably horrified. Watching their years of hard work crumble before their eyes as a machine smirks and takes their jobs. -- Not me. I felt like I opened a shiny door and saw a new world open up. A world where you can give a child with an overactive imagination $20 for a month and they can create a best selling comic book. A world where the stories within can be artistically expressed from a prompt. A world where your only limitations are whether or not you're willing to accept the inevitable. I knew that I had to dig in and master this creative super tool. I just had to be willing to walk through the door..."

**Signature moves:** Pop Culture Anchoring (Miyazaki named in sentence 1, not abstract "AI art"); defamiliarizing verb ("spit out" — mechanistic, irreverent, anti-precious); Vonnegut flat collision ("-- Not me." — three words landing against a paragraph of horror); anaphoric Rule of Three ("A world where... A world where... A world where...") with the third item carrying the emotional weight ("the inevitable"); Thompson factual anchoring ($20 for a month — the smallest concrete number that makes the abstract claim real); personification of the machine ("a machine smirks"); trailing-ellipsis closer (variant of the short Sean closer — door imagery left open).

**Why it works:** the AI version sells the cliché "I'll never forget the day." Sean's version *shows* the day by naming Miyazaki, the phone, the dollar amount, and who got horrified (not him). The triple anaphora is Sedaris mundane-accumulation running on a Beat Flow engine, then snapping to Vonnegut at "Not me." First paragraph lands at ~165 words — right inside the 60-180 Substack-intro band.

### Sample 3 — Agent fleet observation [Twitter long-form]

**AI wrote:** "It's amazing how quickly Claude Code agents have become essential to my daily workflow. Such an exciting time to be building."

**Sean rewrote:** "It's wild how my agent fleet has changed the way I think about work. Whenever I'm presented with a task, I say to my manager 'Yup, you got it'. Then I think to myself, '... I bet I could automate this.'"

**Signature moves:** Screenwriting Cut-To (external dialogue → internal monologue — the gap between what you say and what you think is doing all the work); Self-Deprecation as Structure (outwardly compliant, inwardly scheming); Named ownership ("my agent fleet" vs generic "Claude Code agents"); ellipsis-as-beat-pause closer.

**Why it works:** the AI version is a HubSpot blog tagline. Sean's version is a two-shot scene with dialogue. "Yup, you got it" is the exact phrase, not a paraphrase — uncannily specific. The closer pivots on an ellipsis instead of a period, the closest Sean has to a stage direction in prose.

## Round-2 calibration notes

Observations that should inform future SKILL.md mutations and the next corpus round:

- **`--` is a deliberate em-dash substitute, not a typo.** Visual preference. Preserve as drafted; treat as `—` for any density-feature extraction.
- **LinkedIn is not a separate voice mode.** Covered by Sean Mode at 60% dial. Cross-posts derive from Substack drafts, dialed down. Drop LinkedIn from exercise rotation.
- **Twitter target length: one tweet, one feeling.** Round-1 Twitter samples ran 45-55 words because they were written at "feels right" length with no constraint. With a single-tweet frame, expect 15-30 word samples that compress harder.
- **Mid-action openings are the default, not the exception.** Two of three round-2 samples open inside a specific moment (Miyazaki on a phone, dev at dinner), not with an abstract premise. The Thompson "cold open" is a Sean default across modes, not a Gonzo-only move.
- **Every paragraph contains a named entity.** Confirmed across all three samples (Miyazaki, intent engineering, "my agent fleet", the dev's family dinner). This is existing Sean instinct, and the SKILL.md "named specifics" rule should reflect it as a hard requirement.

## Exercise Rewrites — Round 3 (2026-05-14)

> Constrained-Twitter round. Three Twitter one-liners targeting 15-30 words each, two Substack pieces (opener + closer).
>
> **Three new signature moves discovered this round:** Reader-Dismissal Parenthetical (sample 2), Equation / Formula Defamiliarizer (samples 3 + 5), Inverted Refrain (sample 4). All three appear repeatedly in actual Sean drafting; none currently exist in the `## Sean's Signature Moves` table in SKILL.md. Candidates for the next manual skill edit.

### Sample 1 — Eggs vs agents [Twitter, single tweet]

**AI wrote:** "Working with Claude has completely changed how I approach my work."

**Sean rewrote:** "Claude finished 7 tasks and I didn't finish my eggs. Good ass morning."

**Signature moves:** Hard Cut / Deflation (epic AI achievement → mundane breakfast); Sensory Before Numbers in micro form (specific task count, specific breakfast item); Vonnegut compression closer ("Good ass morning." — 3 words, register-shift to colloquial); Self-Implication (the machine wins, the human is losing at eggs).

**Why it works:** 12 words doing the work of a paragraph. The pivot ("and I didn't finish my eggs") is the entire move — eggs being the most domestic counterweight available to "Claude finished 7 tasks". The closer "Good ass morning." is a colloquial register shift, not "It was a productive morning" or "I'm grateful for the boost."

**Compression check:** Round-1 Twitter samples averaged 45-55 words at "feels right" length. With the single-tweet frame given, Sean lands at 12 words. Constraint produces compression.

### Sample 2 — Layoff Substack opener [Substack, full intensity]

**AI wrote:** "After several incredible years at The Block, I decided it was time to take a leap and bet on myself. The decision wasn't easy, but I knew it was the right one."

**Sean rewrote (as drafted, single paragraph):** "I went through the 6 stages of grief in the span of a week after getting laid off (Yes, 6. There are no rules, nerd). -- The moment repeated in my head each night. 'Unfortunately, I have some bad news...' -- Monday and Tuesday. Denial and shock led to anger and hatred. Then the feeling of being overwhelmed of the unknown and my path suddenly shifting took over. That was Wednesday. I stared into the void from my bed like Tony Soprano on Thursday. When I woke up on Friday, I was able to make some breakfast with my girlfriend and crack a smile. Then on Saturday, I discovered a new stage. I was back at my desk with some green tea, Claude Code written roadmap, and a hopeful future filled with (number 6)... Opportunity."

**Signature moves:** **Reader-Dismissal Parenthetical** (NEW: "Yes, 6. There are no rules, nerd" — addresses the imagined objecting reader, mild-insults them, moves on); Hyper-Specific Anecdote (the exact layoff phrase "Unfortunately, I have some bad news..."); Vonnegut day-of-week mosaic (Monday/Tuesday → Wednesday → Thursday → Friday → Saturday — short declaratives accumulating); Pop Culture Anchoring (Tony Soprano staring into the void); Domestic moment as turning point (girlfriend, breakfast, smile on Friday); Callback Closer with parenthetical hook (returns to "6" with the named answer: "Opportunity"); structural rule-breaking opening ("6 stages of grief" when canon says 5 — leads with the rule-break, then mocks the reader who'd object).

**Why it works:** the AI version sells "leap of faith / bet on myself" — the LinkedIn fiction. Sean overrode the prompt and wrote the truth: he got laid off. That override is corpus-critical data — biographical reality consistently asserts itself over prompt framing. The day-of-week structure is a Vonnegut mosaic deployed inside a Substack frame, and the closing single word "Opportunity." is the kind of one-word landing that makes the whole essay quotable.

**Format note:** As drafted this is one 145-word paragraph with a 27-word last sentence. Voice passes every test; format would fail `substack_format_intro` on both the ≥2 paragraph rule and the ≤12-word closer rule. With three paragraph breaks and "Opportunity." isolated on its own line, the exact same prose passes cleanly:

> I went through the 6 stages of grief in the span of a week after getting laid off (Yes, 6. There are no rules, nerd). -- The moment repeated in my head each night. "Unfortunately, I have some bad news..."
>
> Monday and Tuesday. Denial and shock led to anger and hatred. Then the feeling of being overwhelmed of the unknown and my path suddenly shifting took over. That was Wednesday. I stared into the void from my bed like Tony Soprano on Thursday. When I woke up on Friday, I was able to make some breakfast with my girlfriend and crack a smile. Then on Saturday, I discovered a new stage. I was back at my desk with some green tea, Claude Code written roadmap, and a hopeful future filled with (number 6)...
>
> Opportunity.

**Format vs voice are separable failure modes.** Sean's draft instincts are voice-correct but paragraph-naïve. Either a workflow step or a skill mutation needs to teach paragraph-breaking for impact.

### Sample 3 — PM job market [Twitter, single tweet]

**AI wrote:** "The product manager job market has changed dramatically in the last six months. Adapting to this new landscape is essential."

**Sean rewrote:** "In 2026, PM = You and Claude = Entire P&E department. The only constant is change."

**Signature moves:** **Equation / Formula Defamiliarizer** (NEW: rendering the cultural claim as a mathematical statement — abstract-to-formula reframing); aphoristic closer (using a corporate cliché straight, earned by the absurdity of the equation that precedes it); compression to 16 words.

**Why it works:** the AI version describes the job market changing. Sean's version writes the change as an equation, then closes with a cliché that lands because the equation earned it. The reframe — from "job market changed" to "you are now the entire P&E department" — captures the deeper shift the AI version was gesturing at.

### Sample 4 — Substack closer / 4:45 AM routine [Substack closer]

**AI wrote:** "And so, every morning I rise before the sun and start building. The future belongs to those willing to do the work."

**Sean rewrote:** "I rise before the sun to check in on the agent fleet. I live for these moments. The only sounds I hear are the coffee pot dripping, the birds chirping, and the furious hum of my computer fan putting in the work. And so it begins."

**Signature moves:** Sensory Cascading (Kerouac mechanic); Rule of Three sensory cluster (coffee pot, birds, computer fan); Jewel Center (the computer fan as the irreducible image); Domestic Defamiliarizer applied positively (the computer fan "putting in the work" — human-compliment given to a machine); **Inverted Refrain** (NEW: "And so it begins" inverting Vonnegut's canonical "So it goes" — same cadence, opposite vector. Vonnegut signals resignation; Sean signals anticipation. Counter-evidence to the existing "copying 'So it goes' is Bad Vonnegut" rule: inversion counts as invention).

**Why it works:** the AI version closes with a motivational-poster line. Sean's closes with a sensory cluster anchored on a personified computer fan, then deploys a 4-word refrain that mirrors Vonnegut's cadence but flips its meaning. The closer "And so it begins." is exactly 4 words and lands as a beginning, not an ending — which is what a Substack closer that wants the reader to return should do.

**Closer length:** 4 words. Passes ≤12-word rule.

### Sample 5 — AI hot-takes culture [Twitter, single tweet]

**AI wrote:** "There's so much commentary about AI right now. It's hard to know what to believe anymore."

**Sean rewrote:** "You would think AI Companies were Culinary Schools with the amount they 'COOK'."

**Signature moves:** Equation / Formula Defamiliarizer (same move as Sample 3, here as a categorical comparison rather than equation); Thompson ALL CAPS typographic notation ("'COOK'" in scare quotes — ironic distancing); slang-literalization wordplay (internet-slang "cook" = deliver excellence + literal cook = prepare food → the cooking-school comparison literalizes the slang).

**Why it works:** 14 words. The setup ("AI Companies = Culinary Schools") doesn't make sense literally, which forces the reader to find the slang reference — once found, the scare-quoted 'COOK' lands as the punchline. Wordplay that respects the reader's intelligence.

## Round-3 calibration notes

- **Constraint produces compression.** Round-1 Twitter samples averaged 45-55 words at "feels right" length; round-3 Twitter samples landed at 12, 14, and 16 words with a single-tweet frame. Frame the constraint and Sean compresses.
- **Three new signature moves to add to SKILL.md** (next manual edit): Reader-Dismissal Parenthetical, Equation / Formula Defamiliarizer, Inverted Refrain. All three are documented in the samples above with mechanic + structural shape + example.
- **"Bad Vonnegut" anti-pattern needs nuance.** The current Anti-Patterns table flags "copying 'So it goes' instead of inventing your own refrain" as Bad Vonnegut. Sample 4 ("And so it begins") demonstrates that *inverting* the canonical refrain — same cadence, opposite meaning — counts as invention, not copying. Worth refining the anti-pattern wording when the new moves are added.
- **Format vs voice are separable failure modes.** Sample 2 is voice-correct but paragraph-naïve — single 145-word block with a 27-word closer. The same prose with three paragraph breaks passes every structural check. Future workflow should include a "format pass" after voice is locked.
- **Biographical reality overrides prompt framing.** Asked for "leap and bet on myself," Sean wrote layoff. Substack drafts should accept that the truth of his story will assert itself — better to give the truthful framing as input, or expect override.
- **Machines are personified, not demonized.** Across samples 1, 4, and 5 the machine out-produces Sean (positive), puts in the work (anthropomorphic praise), or "cooks" (slang-positive). The AI-as-threat frame doesn't fit Sean's voice; the Domestic Defamiliarizer is positive-leaning when applied to machines Sean uses.
